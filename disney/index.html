Days held down
They just left. The house goes quiet fast. The first few hours alone are when the routine breaks and the mind starts looking for something to fill the gap.

Morning Intention

I'm not being punished by staying home — I'm proving something to myself. A quiet house is not an empty house. I decide what fills it.

Watch For Today

The first quiet evening
No one's watching the clock but you. That's exactly why tonight's meeting matters most.
Feeling left out or resentful
It's normal to feel a little sad or even resentful that they're at Disney and you're not. Name the feeling — don't medicate it.
"No structure" drift
Without the family's schedule, the day can go shapeless. Shapeless days are when old habits sneak back in.

Today's Missions

Evening Check-In

Full day alone. Work, chores, and free time with nobody to answer to. This is where structure has to be built on purpose, not left to chance.

Morning Intention

Free time is not danger time if I plan it. I'll build today around people and purpose, not around boredom.

Watch For Today

Boredom
An empty afternoon with nothing scheduled is a real trigger. Fill the gaps before they fill themselves.
"No one would know" thinking
Isolation makes secrecy feel possible. That thought itself is the warning sign — bring it into the light immediately.
Extra downtime at night
Without family around at bedtime, nights can run long and unstructured. Set a real bedtime and keep it.

Evening Check-In

Your highest-risk day. This is the midpoint — the novelty of being alone has worn off, missing them hits hardest, and photos or calls from the park can stir up feelings fast. Go in with your plan already made.

Morning Intention

Missing my family is love, not a problem to solve with a drink or a vape. I already know my answer today — I decided this morning.

Watch For Today

Seeing their photos and videos
Watching them have fun without you can bring up sadness, envy, or self-pity fast. Feel it, then call someone — don't sit with it alone.
The long middle-of-trip evening
Day three evenings tend to be the loneliest. This is exactly why tonight's in-person meeting is non-negotiable.
Self-pity spiral
"I always miss out" thinking is a trigger dressed as a feeling. Write it down, then read your Toolkit reading.

Today's Missions

Evening Check-In

They come home today. Excitement and anticipation are their own kind of high — and the urge to "celebrate" their return can sneak in disguised as joy.

Morning Intention

I want to greet my family exactly as clear-headed as I've been all week. That's the gift I'm giving them at the door.

Watch For Today

"Let's celebrate" urge
Their homecoming is genuinely happy news. Happy feelings can trigger just as hard as hard ones — plan how you'll celebrate before they walk in.
Waiting-around anxiety
Watching the clock for their flight or drive home can stir up restlessness. Keep the day scheduled until they're actually home.
Wanting credit
You did something real this week. You don't need to announce it to feel it — but it's fair to tell your sponsor.

Why I'm Doing This

Ride It Out

Follow the circle. Breathe in as it grows, hold, breathe out as it shrinks. Cravings are like waves — they peak and pass in under two minutes if you don't feed them.

Breathe

Serenity Prayer

God, grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom to know the difference.

Say it slow. Say it twice if you need to. It's short on purpose — so you can carry it with you into any quiet room, any long evening alone.

One-Minute Reading

You are not the one missing out this week. You are the one showing your family — and yourself — what steady looks like. That is not a smaller trip. It might be the most important one you take this year.

Recovery doesn't pause when the house gets quiet — it's proven there. One meeting, one night, one decision at a time.
